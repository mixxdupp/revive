ID: food-universal-edibility
NAME: Universal Edibility Test
SOURCE: https://archive.org/search?query=FM+21-76+Survival+Manual
  STEP 1: Test only ONE plant part at a time (leaf, root, fruit, stem).
  STEP 2: Rub on inner wrist for 15 minutes. Wait 45 minutes for reaction.
  STEP 3: Touch to lip corner for 3 minutes. Then tongue tip for 15 minutes.
  STEP 4: Chew a small piece for 15 minutes without swallowing.
  STEP 5: If no ill effects after 8 hours, eat 1/4 cup. Wait another 8 hours.

ID: food-rose-hip-tea
NAME: Rose Hip Tea
SOURCE: https://openlibrary.org/isbn/9780395926222
  STEP 1: Identify rose hips: red-orange fruit at the base of wild rose flowers, appearing in late summer through winter.
  STEP 2: Harvest hips when red and slightly soft. They sweeten after the first frost.
  STEP 3: Split hips open and remove the seeds and fine hairs inside. The hairs are irritating if swallowed.
  STEP 4: Crush or chop the hip flesh. Steep in hot (not boiling) water for 15-20 minutes.
  STEP 5: Rose hips contain 20x more vitamin C than oranges by weight. Critical for preventing scurvy in long-term survival.

ID: food-organ-meat-nutrition
NAME: Organ Meat Nutrition
SOURCE: https://archive.org/search?query=FM+21-76+Survival+Manual
  STEP 1: Liver: the most nutrient-dense organ. Provides massive amounts of vitamins A, B12, iron, and protein.
  STEP 2: Heart: lean muscle tissue, high in protein, iron, and CoQ10. Treat like a steak — slice and cook.
  STEP 3: Kidneys: high in B12 and selenium. Soak in cold water for 30 minutes first to reduce strong flavor.
  STEP 4: Stomach and intestines: clean thoroughly, boil for 30+ minutes. Good source of fat and protein.
  STEP 5: Bone marrow: crack long bones and roast near fire. Marrow is calorie-dense and rich in healthy fats.

ID: food-plantain-wild

  STEP 1: Identify: rosette of broad oval leaves with prominent parallel veins, growing close to the ground.

ID: food-acorn-leaching-methods
NAME: Acorn Leaching Methods
SOURCE: https://openlibrary.org/isbn/9781440579776
  STEP 1: Crack acorn shells with a rock. Remove meats and discard any with insect holes or dark spots.
  STEP 2: Cold water method (best): crush meats, submerge in cold flowing water (stream) for 24-48 hours.
  STEP 3: Hot water method (faster): boil crushed acorn meat, pour off brown water, add fresh water, repeat 5-7 times.
  STEP 4: Taste test: a small piece should be mild and nutty with no bitterness. If bitter, continue leaching.
  STEP 5: Use leached acorn meal to make flatbread, porridge, or thickener for stews. Dry for long-term storage.

ID: food-sap-collection
NAME: Tree Sap Collection
SOURCE: https://openlibrary.org/isbn/9781440579776
  STEP 1: Tappable trees: maple, birch, walnut, sycamore. Sap flows in late winter/early spring when nights are freezing and days thaw.
  STEP 2: Drill or cut a 1-2 inch deep hole at a slight upward angle, 3 feet from the ground.

  STEP 5: Drink sap directly — it is mildly sweet and hydrating. Boil down for syrup (40:1 ratio for maple).

ID: food-hand-fishing
NAME: Hand Fishing (Noodling)
SOURCE: https://archive.org/search?query=FM+21-76+Survival+Manual
  STEP 1: Wade slowly through shallow water near undercut banks, submerged logs, and rock crevices.
  STEP 2: Feel slowly under banks and into holes with your hands. Fish hiding in holes will not flee — they are cornered.
  STEP 3: When you feel a fish, slide your hand forward to the gills. Grip firmly behind the gill plate.
  STEP 4: For flatfish (catfish, flounder): pin against the bottom with both hands, then scoop upward.
  STEP 5: Caution: be aware of snakes, turtles, and crayfish in the same holes. Feel carefully before grabbing.

ID: food-rock-boiling-cooking
NAME: Rock Boiling
SOURCE: https://openlibrary.org/isbn/9781440579776
  STEP 1: Create a watertight container: birch bark folded box, hollowed log, animal stomach, or depression in rock.
  STEP 2: Heat rocks in your fire for 30+ minutes until they glow or hiss when sprinkled with water.
  STEP 3: Use wooden tongs (split green sticks) to transfer hot rocks from fire to water container.
  STEP 4: Each rock heats the water significantly. 3-4 rocks will bring a quart to a rolling boil.
  STEP 5: Rock boiling can cook stews, soups, render fat, sterilize water, and process plant foods.

ID: food-nettle-harvest

SOURCE: https://openlibrary.org/isbn/9780395926222
  STEP 1: Identify: tall plant with opposite, serrated, heart-shaped leaves. Covered in fine stinging hairs.
  STEP 2: Harvest using gloves, thick cloth, or by gripping the stem firmly (the stingers fold flat under firm pressure).

ID: food-bait-collection

  STEP 1: Worms: drive a stake into moist ground and rub another stick across the top. Vibrations bring worms up.

ID: food-bone-grease
NAME: Bone Grease Rendering
SOURCE: https://archive.org/search?query=FM+21-76+Survival+Manual
  STEP 1: Crack long bones into 2-inch pieces using rocks. Smash thoroughly — expose the marrow and spongy interior.
  STEP 2: Place crushed bones in a pot or rock-boiling container with water. Bring to a sustained boil.

  STEP 4: Let rendered fat cool and solidify. It stores for weeks in a cool, sealed container.
  STEP 5: Use bone grease for cooking, pemmican making, waterproofing leather, and skin protection.

  STEP 1: Pinch the skin at the middle of the back and make a small cut through the skin only — not the muscle.
  STEP 2: Insert two fingers into the cut and pull in opposite directions — the skin peels off like a shirt.

  STEP 4: Minimal flavor, gummy texture, but prevents starvation.
