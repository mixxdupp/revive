ID: fire-bowdrill
NAME: Bow Drill Fire
SOURCE: https://openlibrary.org/isbn/9781440579776
  STEP 1: Assemble 4 components: fireboard, spindle, bow, handhold.
  STEP 2: Carve a notch in the fireboard to collect ember dust.
  STEP 3: Wrap the bowstring around the spindle once.
  STEP 4: Apply downward pressure with handhold and saw the bow back and forth.
  STEP 5: When heavy smoke pours from the notch, stop. The ember is born.

ID: fire-handdrill
NAME: Hand Drill Fire
SOURCE: https://archive.org/search?query=FM+21-76+Survival+Manual
  STEP 1: Find a straight, dry spindle 18-24 inches long, pencil-thick.
  STEP 2: Use the same fireboard setup as bow drill with V-notch.
  STEP 3: Roll the spindle between your palms while pressing down.
  STEP 4: Continue until ember forms in the notch dust.

ID: fire-extinguish
NAME: Properly Extinguishing a Fire
SOURCE: https://www.animatedknots.com/
  STEP 1: Let the fire burn down to coals — stop adding fuel well before departure.
  STEP 2: Sprinkle water on the coals — do NOT dump.
  STEP 3: Stir the ashes and wet every surface.
  STEP 4: Hold your hand over the ashes at 6 inches. If warm, repeat.

ID: fire-wet-wood-processing
NAME: Wet Wood Processing
SOURCE: https://openlibrary.org/isbn/9781440579776
  STEP 1: Never use wood from the ground in wet conditions — it absorbs moisture like a sponge.
  STEP 2: Split wet wood to expose the dry interior. Even soaked logs have dry centers.
  STEP 3: Shave feather sticks from the dry inner wood — thin curls that catch fire easily.
  STEP 4: Collect birch bark — it contains oils that burn even when wet.
  STEP 5: Use a 'fire sandwich': lay wet wood near the fire to dry while burning dry wood. Rotate as wood dries.

ID: fire-rain-fire-management

SOURCE: https://openlibrary.org/isbn/9780062378071
  STEP 1: Build your fire on a raised platform of green logs if ground is saturated.
  STEP 2: Create a rain cover: lean a bark slab or tarp at an angle over the fire, high enough to avoid catching fire.
  STEP 3: Maintain a larger fire than usual — more mass resists rain extinguishment.

ID: fire-fire-safety-camp
NAME: Camp Fire Safety
SOURCE: https://archive.org/search?query=FM+21-76+Survival+Manual
  STEP 1: Clear all leaves, needles, and dry debris in a 10-foot diameter circle around the fire.
  STEP 2: Build a fire ring from rocks — do NOT use river rocks (they can explode from trapped moisture).
  STEP 3: Keep the fire only as large as you need. A fist-sized fire boils water; a torso-sized fire heats a shelter.
  STEP 4: Never leave a fire unattended. When leaving, drown it — stir — drown again — feel with hand.
  STEP 5: In windy conditions, keep fire LOW or dig a Dakota fire hole to contain flames.

ID: fire-underground-fire
NAME: Underground Fire (Dakota Hole)
SOURCE: https://archive.org/search?query=FM+21-76+Survival+Manual
  STEP 1: Dig a main fire pit — 12 inches wide, 12 inches deep. Flare the bottom slightly wider.
  STEP 2: Dig a ventilation tunnel angled from 12 inches upwind to the bottom of the fire pit.
  STEP 3: Build your fire in the bottom of the main pit. Start small and build up.
  STEP 4: Advantages: minimal smoke, low visibility, wind-resistant, concentrated heat for cooking.
  STEP 5: Place a flat rock or green wood grill across the top for cooking. The pit concentrates heat upward.

ID: fire-magnifying-glass

  STEP 1: Any convex lens works: magnifying glass, binocular lens, eyeglasses (farsighted only), camera lens, water-filled bag.

SOURCE: https://openlibrary.org/isbn/9781440579776
  STEP 1: Birch bark contains betulin oil — naturally waterproof and highly flammable even when wet.

ID: fire-night-fire-management
NAME: Night Fire Management
SOURCE: https://archive.org/search?query=FM+21-76+Survival+Manual
  STEP 1: Build a large coal bed before sleeping — feed the fire heavily with hardwood 30 minutes before bedtime.
  STEP 2: Use the long-log method: place 2-3 large logs side by side on the coal bed.
  STEP 3: Bank the fire: push ash and coals against the logs on the sleeping side. Reduce open flames.
  STEP 4: Set a mental alarm or buddy system to check the fire every 2-3 hours.
