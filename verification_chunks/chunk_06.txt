ID: psych-trauma-response-phases
NAME: Trauma Response Phases
SOURCE: https://archive.org/search?query=FM+21-76+Survival+Manual
  STEP 1: Phase 1 — Impact: shock, disbelief, numbness. The brain shuts down non-essential functions. This is normal.
  STEP 2: Phase 2 — Recoil: reality sets in. Emotions flood: fear, anger, grief, guilt. Physical symptoms appear.
  STEP 3: Phase 3 — Adjustment: begins within hours to days. Problem-solving resumes. New routines form.
  STEP 4: Phase 4 — Reconstruction: days to weeks. Long-term coping develops. Identity integrates the experience.
  STEP 5: Key insight: allowing emotions to FLOW rather than suppressing them speeds recovery. Cry if you need to. Then act.

ID: psych-fear-vs-panic
NAME: Fear vs. Panic Protocol
SOURCE: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilderness_first_responder
  STEP 1: Acknowledge the difference: Fear is a survival tool that heightens senses. Panic is a loss of rational control.
  STEP 2: Identify panic somatic markers: tunnel vision, hyperventilation, trembling, inability to process instructions.
  STEP 3: Execute a physical interrupter: splash cold water on your face, bite your lip, snap a rubber band on your wrist.

ID: psych-sleep-deprivation-coping
NAME: Sleep Deprivation Coping
SOURCE: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilderness_first_responder
  STEP 1: Understand the timeline: at 24 hours awake, cognitive impairment equals a 0.10 BAC (legally drunk).
  STEP 2: Implement the 2-Person Rule for all critical decisions. No one decides to move, cross water, or abandon gear alone.
  STEP 3: Use caffeine or stimulants strategically, not constantly. Save them for when physical movement is mandatory.
  STEP 4: Take 'combat naps.' A 20-minute nap clears adenosine (sleep pressure chemical) from the brain, restoring temporary function.
  STEP 5: Recognize microsleeps. If you catch your head nodding or realize you don't remember the last 10 seconds of walking, STOP.
