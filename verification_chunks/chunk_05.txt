ID: nav-north-star
NAME: Finding North Star (Polaris)
SOURCE: https://openlibrary.org/isbn/9781440579776
  STEP 1: Find the Big Dipper (7 bright stars in a ladle shape).
  STEP 2: Follow the two 'pointer stars' at the end of the cup upward.
  STEP 3: Polaris is at the end of the Little Dipper's handle.
  STEP 4: Face Polaris. You are facing TRUE north.

ID: nav-southern-cross
NAME: Southern Cross Navigation
SOURCE: https://openlibrary.org/isbn/9781440579776
  STEP 1: Find the Southern Cross — 4 bright stars in a cross/kite shape.
  STEP 2: Extend the long axis of the cross 4.5× its length.
  STEP 3: Draw a perpendicular line from the midpoint of the pointer stars.
  STEP 4: Where the two lines intersect is roughly south.

ID: nav-stick-shadow
NAME: Stick & Shadow Method
SOURCE: https://archive.org/search?query=FM+21-76+Survival+Manual
  STEP 1: Push a straight stick vertically into the ground.
  STEP 2: Mark the tip of the shadow with a stone.
  STEP 3: Wait 15-20 minutes. Mark the new shadow tip.
  STEP 4: Draw a line between the two marks. This is your east-west line.

ID: nav-sun-position
NAME: Sun Position Navigation
SOURCE: https://openlibrary.org/isbn/9781440579776
  STEP 1: The sun rises in the EAST and sets in the WEST.
  STEP 2: At solar noon (highest point), the sun is due SOUTH (Northern Hemisphere).
  STEP 3: Estimate time from sun position for rough direction.

ID: nav-watch-compass
NAME: Analog Watch Compass
SOURCE: https://www.ngdc.noaa.gov/geomag/
  STEP 1: Hold the watch horizontal. Point the HOUR HAND at the sun.
  STEP 2: Find the midpoint between the hour hand and 12 o'clock.
  STEP 3: That midpoint direction is SOUTH (Northern Hemisphere).

ID: nav-compass-use
NAME: Using a Magnetic Compass
SOURCE: https://www.ngdc.noaa.gov/geomag/
  STEP 1: Hold compass level. Let needle settle.
  STEP 2: Rotate the bezel until N aligns with the red needle.
  STEP 3: Read your desired bearing. Point the direction-of-travel arrow that way.
  STEP 4: Pick a distant landmark on your bearing and walk to it. Repeat.

ID: nav-map-reading
NAME: Map & Compass Navigation
SOURCE: https://www.ngdc.noaa.gov/geomag/
  STEP 1: Orient the map: align map's north arrow with compass north.
  STEP 2: Identify 2-3 visible terrain features on your map.
  STEP 3: Take compass bearings to each feature.
  STEP 4: Where the bearing lines intersect on the map is your position.

ID: nav-natural-indicators
NAME: Natural Direction Indicators
SOURCE: https://archive.org/search?query=FM+21-76+Survival+Manual
  STEP 1: Spider webs are often built on the south side of trees.
  STEP 2: Snow melts faster on south-facing slopes.
  STEP 3: Tree growth rings are wider on the south side (Northern Hemisphere).
  STEP 4: DO NOT rely on moss — the 'moss grows on the north side' myth is unreliable.

ID: nav-star-movement
NAME: Direction from Star Movement
SOURCE: https://openlibrary.org/isbn/9780062378071
  STEP 1: Fix a reference point: lie down and align a star with a stick or rock edge.
  STEP 2: If the star moved up: you're facing EAST.
  STEP 3: If it moved down: you're facing WEST.
  STEP 4: If it moved right: you're facing SOUTH. Left: NORTH.

ID: nav-dead-reckoning
NAME: Dead Reckoning
SOURCE: https://openlibrary.org/isbn/9780062378071
  STEP 1: Calibrate your pace count: walk 100 meters and count steps.
  STEP 2: Set your compass bearing. Count paces as you walk.
  STEP 3: At each pace milestone, plot your position on the map.
  STEP 4: Correct for terrain: adjust pace for slope, vegetation, and footing.

ID: nav-gps-phone
NAME: GPS Navigation on Phone
SOURCE: https://openlibrary.org/isbn/9780062378071
  STEP 1: GPS works WITHOUT cell service or WiFi.
  STEP 2: Download offline maps BEFORE your trip.
  STEP 3: Your position shows as a blue dot on offline maps.
  STEP 4: Mark waypoints for camp, water, and vehicle.

ID: nav-lost-procedure
NAME: Lost Procedure (STOP)
SOURCE: https://archive.org/search?query=FM+21-76+Survival+Manual
  STEP 1: STOP. Do not take another step.
  STEP 2: THINK. When was the last time you knew where you were?
  STEP 3: OBSERVE. Climb to high ground for visibility.
  STEP 4: PLAN. Either backtrack to your last known position or signal for help.

SOURCE: https://www.ready.gov/

  STEP 2: Stay near the water but travel on the bank.
  STEP 3: Watch for signs of civilization: fences, bridges, trash, cut stumps.

ID: nav-terrain-association

  STEP 2: Use handrails: features that run parallel to your direction.
  STEP 3: Use backstops: features that cross your path and signal 'too far.'

  STEP 2: Northern Hemisphere: bisect the angle between the hour hand and 12 o'clock. That's south.
  STEP 3: Southern Hemisphere: point 12 at the sun. Bisect between 12 and the hour hand. That's north.

ID: nav-wind-direction-feel
NAME: Wind Direction by Feel
SOURCE: https://archive.org/search?query=FM+21-76+Survival+Manual
  STEP 1: Wet your finger and hold it up. The cool side faces the wind direction.
  STEP 2: Note the wind direction relative to the time of day. Morning winds often shift from continental patterns.
  STEP 3: Drop grass or light debris. Watch the drift direction to confirm wind observation.
  STEP 4: Smoke from a fire shows wind direction at different heights — ground wind may differ from canopy level.
  STEP 5: In mountains, wind typically flows upslope during the day and downslope at night.

ID: nav-cloud-type-identification
NAME: Cloud Type Navigation
SOURCE: https://www.weather.gov/
  STEP 1: Cumulus clouds (puffy, flat-bottomed) over open land during daytime often indicate thermal updrafts from solid ground.
  STEP 2: Lenticular clouds (lens-shaped) indicate mountains downwind. They form on the lee side of high terrain.
  STEP 3: Dark, flat-bottomed clouds on the horizon may indicate water beneath (reflection of water surface).
  STEP 4: Cloud movement shows upper-level wind direction — this is more consistent than ground-level wind.
  STEP 5: Towering cumulonimbus (thunderhead) clouds = severe weather approaching. Move perpendicular to their travel path.

ID: nav-insect-behavior
NAME: Insect Behavior Navigation
SOURCE: https://archive.org/search?query=FM+21-76+Survival+Manual
  STEP 1: Bees: in the morning, bees fly FROM their hive toward water and flowers. In evening, they return.
  STEP 2: Ants: follow ant trails — they often lead to water sources. Ants build mounds on specific sides.
  STEP 3: Mosquitoes and midges: heavy swarms near sunset indicate water within 200-400 meters.
  STEP 4: Spiders: web orientation can indicate prevailing wind. Spiders avoid building webs facing the wind.
  STEP 5: Fireflies: common near water sources and in moist areas. Their density increases closer to streams and ponds.

ID: nav-animal-trail-following
NAME: Animal Trail Navigation
SOURCE: https://archive.org/search?query=FM+21-76+Survival+Manual
  STEP 1: Game trails that converge and widen indicate a water source ahead. Multiple trails merging = high probability.
  STEP 2: Well-worn trails with layered hoofprints indicate a regularly used, reliable path.
  STEP 3: Trails along ridgelines provide the easiest travel and best visibility.
  STEP 4: Downhill game trails usually lead to water. Uphill trails often lead to bedding areas or lookout points.
  STEP 5: CAUTION: animal trails may pass through hazardous terrain that animals navigate easily but humans cannot.

ID: nav-snow-drift-orientation
NAME: Snow Drift Orientation
SOURCE: https://archive.org/search?query=FM+21-76+Survival+Manual
  STEP 1: Snow drifts build on the leeward (downwind) side of obstacles — rocks, trees, buildings.
  STEP 2: Sastrugi (wind-sculpted snow ridges) align with the prevailing wind direction.
  STEP 3: In the Northern Hemisphere, south-facing slopes have less snow (more sun melting).
  STEP 4: Tree wells (deep holes around tree bases) are asymmetric — deeper on the side sheltered from wind/sun.
  STEP 5: Combine multiple snow indicators for reliable direction-finding in featureless white terrain.

ID: nav-lichen-growth
NAME: Lichen Growth Direction
SOURCE: https://openlibrary.org/isbn/9781440579776
  STEP 1: In the Northern Hemisphere, lichen grows thickest on the north side of trees and rocks.
  STEP 2: This is opposite in the Southern Hemisphere — thickest lichen growth on the south side.
  STEP 3: Check multiple trees and rocks to establish a pattern — individual specimens can be unreliable.
  STEP 4: Algae (green) on tree trunks follows the same pattern as lichen — thicker on the shaded pole-facing side.
  STEP 5: Lichen direction-finding is a supplementary method — cross-reference with sun, stars, or wind indicators.

ID: nav-bird-flight-patterns
NAME: Bird Flight Pattern Navigation
SOURCE: https://archive.org/search?query=FM+21-76+Survival+Manual
  STEP 1: At dawn, most land birds fly FROM their roost toward water and feeding areas.
  STEP 2: At dusk, birds return to their roosts — following evening flight often leads to sheltered areas.
  STEP 3: Seabirds fly out to sea in the morning to fish and return to land at dusk.
  STEP 4: Circling birds often indicate food below: raptors circle over prey, vultures circle over carrion or thermals.
  STEP 5: Geese and ducks flying in V-formations indicate seasonal direction — south in fall, north in spring (Northern Hemisphere).

ID: nav-pressure-detection
NAME: Barometric Pressure Detection
SOURCE: https://www.weather.gov/
  STEP 1: Ear pressure: if your ears feel 'full' or you need to pop them, barometric pressure is dropping — bad weather coming.
  STEP 2: Campfire smoke: smoke rising straight up = high pressure (good weather). Smoke that sinks or swirls = low pressure (storm coming).
  STEP 3: Sound traveling farther than usual indicates low pressure and approaching weather change.
  STEP 4: Joint pain or old injury aches intensifying can indicate dropping pressure (scientifically debated but widely reported).

ID: nav-ocean-swel-reading
NAME: Ocean Swell Reading
SOURCE: https://www.uscgboating.org
  STEP 1: Polynesian navigators used swell direction as a primary compass. Swells are ocean waves generated by distant storms, unaffected by local wind.
  STEP 2: Observe the ocean surface. Ignore the chaotic top layer of waves. Look for the underlying, rhythmic rise and fall of the water.
  STEP 3: Identify the dominant swell. The dominant swell is usually the largest and most consistent. Note its direction relative to your boat.
  STEP 4: Check for a secondary swell crossing the primary swell. This creates an interference pattern (a 'grid' on the water).
  STEP 5: Watch for swell refraction around islands. Swells bend around landmasses.
